Interview Prep Guide

Ace at your technical interviews and get one step closer to landing your dream job

- by Yasier Arafat

This guide is designed to help you successfully prepare for your first interview with a hiring person, a hiring manager, or a member of a non-professional team. You can use this guide whether you are new to negotiating, not chatting for a long time, or just want to refresh your negotiating skills. We will discuss key interview preparation techniques ranging from focusing on your thinking to learning how to present your previous knowledge in the best possible way.

Know Your Audience

Spend a few hours learning all you can about the company — from as many sources as possible. Chat with friends and contacts, read the latest news, and, yes, spend some time on Google. Often, candidates will simply look at the information the company is publishing through the website and social media, but fail to take an in-depth look at what others are saying. By doing so, you will get a bigger picture about the company (and any negative pressures).

Get a sense of “who” the company is and how you can incorporate the same personality into your conversation. Start by reading the company blog and Facebook page — the tone of company content on these sites will speak volumes. Or, try reading individual staff blogs to find out what kind of people are working (and effective) there. Twitter can be a great resource because you can see what the company and its employees are talking about. Are they ridiculing each other? Feel free to throw in a few jokes as you meet people. Are they sending a storm about an event or product launch? Use it as a conversation starter.

No matter what role you negotiate — engineering, marketing, marketing — you should use the product before your first interview (and appropriately, a few times). Once you are hired, your goal will be to create the number of people using that product, and being a user yourself is the first step.

Make sure you have access to your network to find current and former employees to talk to, and they are often your best intel source of what it really is like to work for the company.

Before your interview, get a list of people you meet in the company. Then learn more about them — including the type of behavior that they are likely to find interesting or objectionable. Lastly, prepare specific questions for each interviewer: Ask for details about his or her focus on the company, discuss current events about his or her special job, or point out similar interests that you know you have outside the office.

Different companies use different types of negotiations, so ask what you will deal with. For example, some companies will ask trial questions or brainstorming while others will offer a standard set of general interviews and leadership questions. Asking an employee or HR contact about the interview format in advance is a fair game. And if you know, investing time to get used to this style can make a huge difference.



Anticipate Discussion Questions

Even if you are a well-trained interviewer, it is important to spend time thinking about what skills, achievements, and interview answers will best suit your interviewers. Your management skills? Your ingenuity? The examples you share will be slightly different everywhere you talk. Have the answer “Tell me about yourself” ready to go. Interviewees often ask her, and she wants to make sure she attaches this first part of the interview.

Don't be fooled by the old story "What's your biggest weakness?" One ridiculous way: Think of something you are struggling with but working to improve. For example, you may not have been strong in public speaking, but in the past few years, you have taken on leadership roles and have volunteered to conduct meetings to help you feel more comfortable talking to the crowd.

You can easily find a list of common interview questions — but do not prepare by writing down your entire answer; instead, write down a few notes or points for the letters and keep them in the interview itself. You will ensure that you close the basics — without reading the text.

Don't forget about the numbers! Finding specific numbers, percentages, increments, or quotes that you can use when talking about your responsibilities and accomplishments makes the contract fun and helps you tell your employer why you are so good. (Here's how to put them on, even if you don't work with numbers.)

You may be asked why you are interested in this role with a particular company. (And if you can't answer that question, you don't have to be in a conversation!) So to make sure you can, think about why you are interested in this job and identify a few key factors that make you a good fit. about you and how it relates to what motivates you (e.g., “I love customer support because I love the constant communication and satisfaction that comes with helping someone solve a problem”).

Do not think about how you will answer certain questions; make it a practice to look in the mirror and answer them aloud. This preparation activity will help you to clarify your thoughts and make you more comfortable during the interview.

Have as many humorous conversations as possible with a friend. You will be better off answering, "What can you replace?" 100 times you do more than the first, right?

Prepare a few clever questions to use when it is your turn to ask. Make sure they are the ones that show you have been paying attention and doing your homework when it comes to researching the company and the specific job you are looking for.



Dress up Properly

Plan the perfect costume for the interview. For companies with a business or unusual dress code keep your appearance basic and orderly in an initial interview. Not sure what to wear? If you have a contact person or a friend who works for the company you are interviewing with, see if he or she can explain what people are wearing. If you do not have that luxury, look at photos on social media (or their Muse! Profile), or join a coffee shop across the street a day or two in advance and try to see what people are wearing. . Make sure your clothes are neat, pressed, and done properly (modern fit is best). People often have their “chat room” sitting in their closet a few years ago, and they dust you off at this event you don't want to be that person.

Don't forget about the little things: Fill your shoes, check your loose hem, and make sure your nails look neat. These are things you don't think people see, but they see!

Make a little massage, because looking good helps you feel better. If that means you need a haircut, a haircut, a razor blade, or a new interview dress, then do it by all means! Feeling good about yourself can boost your self-confidence and it probably does not have to tell you that self-confidence is the key to getting your dream job.

Print five copies of your resume. You never know who you will meet, and you want your resume ready to go when you are asked.

Prepare a reference list, whether you think you will be asked or not. For each reference, include the name, title, organization, category or department, telephone number, email address, and a brief description of the relationship (e.g., "Carlton was my team leader for two years, where we worked together. Launch of four major brands").

Prepare your wallet, backpack, or wallet. Any bag you carry should be large enough to hold your daily essentials, as well as things to discuss, such as additional CVs and brochures, as well as a special emergency kit with items you may need in an unexpected situation (think: Band-Aids, color stick, umbrella , and respiratory sticks). Keep your Head In the Right Place


Spend a lot of time before the interview not repeating the questions, but think about the chronology of your work so far. If you know your story inside and out, it is very easy to use examples in almost any interview question.

Preparing for a technical interview? Start preparing early. Working with a preparation book or sample questions will not only give you good practice, but will also put you in the right frame of mind for solving problems.

Come up with a phrase that will help you avoid the dead spirit if you need time to stop and gather your thoughts. Two strategies work well to repeat a question thoughtfully before answering or saying (slightly), “Now, that is a good question. I think I should say… "

Analyze the meaning of a particular body language. Be careful what you say about your situation and your situation — and make sure it is good. (For example, constantly crossing your arms and legs sends a message that you are not closed or that you feel secure.) Carefully consider your movements early so as not to be distracted (or interrupted) during the interview.

Use an interview cheat sheet to gather all the information you need to remember, make notes of what you want to say and ask, and check out all the important things you will bring to the interview. Publish one for every interview, read it in the morning, and you will be ready to go! (We made you one right here!)

Oh, and get some sleep. This sounds like something your mom can tell you, but there are a few things that will take you out of your game like sleep deprivation.
